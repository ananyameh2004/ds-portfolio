Housing Affordability
in the United States

Tracking the widening gap between home prices, household income, and mortgage rates — 1984 to 2024 — using FRED economic data.

Week 10

Topic Selection

I'm working on this project solo. After considering three possible topics — housing affordability, college tuition trends, and fast food nutrition pricing — I chose housing affordability. It's one of the most consequential economic issues facing people my age as we start thinking about where to live and work.

My plan is to combine FRED national data on home prices and mortgage rates with Zillow or Census data for city-level granularity to answer questions about which cities have seen the fastest deterioration in affordability, and whether mid-sized cities saw a lasting COVID-era price shift.

Week 11

Data Sources & Wrangling

I settled on three FRED series: MSPUS (median new home sale price, quarterly), MEHOINUSA646N (real median household income, annual), and MORTGAGE30US (30-year fixed mortgage rate, weekly). I originally planned to use the NAR Housing Affordability Index but it's copyright-restricted to only the last 13 months, so I built my own affordability metrics from scratch.

The main wrangling challenge was reconciling three different time frequencies. I resampled the weekly mortgage data to quarterly averages, forward-filled the annual income into quarterly observations, and merged everything on a shared quarterly index from 1984 to 2024. I then engineered two metrics: a price-to-income ratio and a monthly payment-to-income ratio using the standard amortization formula with 20% down.

Key Findings

  • The post-2020 affordability collapse is historically distinct. The early 1980s had worse payment burdens, but those were driven purely by high interest rates on low prices. The 2022–2024 period is the first time both prices and rates were elevated simultaneously.
  • The 2008 crash gave only temporary relief. The price-to-income ratio fell from ~5.7× back to ~3.5× by 2012, then resumed climbing almost immediately. By 2022 it had exceeded the pre-crash peak.
  • Falling mortgage rates masked the problem for a decade. From 2010–2021, rates fell from ~5% to under 3%, keeping monthly burdens manageable even as prices climbed. Once rates reversed in 2022, the full scale of the price gap became apparent overnight.
  • Income has grown, but nowhere near fast enough. Real median income rose ~60% from 1984 to 2024. But median new home prices rose over 400% in nominal terms — more than 4× the rate of income growth.
  • The 3× benchmark is effectively obsolete. The ratio has not been below 3× since the early 1990s. Whether this reflects a structural supply failure or a prolonged bubble is a key open question.

Limitations

National aggregates only

All three series are U.S.-wide. City-level variation — the core of my original research questions — is invisible in this data.

New construction only

MSPUS covers newly built homes which skew higher-end. Affordability estimates may be slightly more pessimistic than the broader resale market.

20% down assumed

Increasingly unrealistic for first-time buyers. Those putting down less face higher monthly costs and PMI, making the actual burden worse than shown.

Renters excluded

About 35% of U.S. households rent and are entirely absent. The median income figure also hides deteriorating affordability for lower-income households.

Week 14

Project Update & Final Plan

With about a week and a half left before the final project is due, I have a clearer picture of what's done and what still needs to happen. The core analysis is largely in place — the three FRED datasets are cleaned, merged, and producing the affordability metrics I set out to build. The exploratory visualizations from Week 12 confirmed the main story: home prices have outpaced income by a wide margin over the past four decades, the early-2020s surge was historically unusual, and the combination of high prices and high interest rates after 2022 created an affordability crunch with no real precedent in the data.

New Technique
Linear Regression for Trend Analysis
The new technique I'm planning to implement is linear regression on time series data — specifically, fitting a regression line to the price-to-income ratio over time to model its long-run trend and identify quarters where affordability was significantly above or below that trend. This lets me say something more precise than "prices went up" — I can quantify the rate of deterioration and flag the specific periods (like 2020–2022) where affordability broke away from the historical trend line the most.

I'm learning this through a combination of approaches: reviewing the scikit-learn documentation for LinearRegression, working through the relevant DataCamp module on regression in Python, and reading web articles on applying OLS regression to economic time series.

Communication Strategy

Narrative structure: The project will be organized around a single central question — "How did housing go from something most working Americans could afford to something many cannot?" — answered in three acts. The first establishes the historical baseline from the 1980s and 1990s. The second traces the deterioration through the 2000s bubble, the 2008 crash, and the low-rate recovery. The third focuses on the post-2020 surge and what the data says about where things stand now.

Visuals: I'll lead with the indexed growth chart since it's the most immediately striking — the visual gap between the home price and income lines is impossible to miss. The price-to-income ratio chart comes second to put a concrete number on that gap. The payment-to-income chart comes third because it brings in the mortgage rate dimension and shows why 2022–2024 is uniquely difficult. The regression trendline will appear alongside the price-to-income chart as a fourth visual.

Presentation format: I plan to present directly from this project webpage rather than making a separate slide deck. The page is already structured as a narrative with sections, and the interactive Plotly charts work better as live demos than as screenshots in slides. I'll walk through each section during the presentation and use the hover tooltips to highlight specific data points in real time.
