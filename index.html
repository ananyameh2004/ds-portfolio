Data Science Final Project

Housing Affordability
in the United States

Tracking the widening gap between home prices, household income, and mortgage rates — 1984 to 2024 — using FRED economic data.

Week 10

Topic Selection

I'm working on this project solo. After considering three possible topics — housing affordability, college tuition trends, and fast food nutrition pricing — I chose housing affordability. It's one of the most consequential economic issues facing people my age as we start thinking about where to live and work, so it felt like the right project to actually dig into the data behind it.

My plan is to combine FRED national data on home prices and mortgage rates with Zillow or Census data for city-level granularity. The central questions I want to answer are about which cities have seen the fastest deterioration in affordability, and whether mid-sized cities saw a lasting COVID-era price shift relative to large coastal metros.

Week 11

Data Sources & Wrangling

I settled on three FRED series as my primary datasets: MSPUS (median new home sale price, quarterly since 1963), MEHOINUSA646N (real median household income, annual since 1984), and MORTGAGE30US (30-year fixed mortgage rate, weekly since 1971). I originally planned to use the NAR Housing Affordability Index from FRED as a cross-check, but it's copyright-restricted to only the last 13 months. That pushed me to construct my own affordability measures from scratch — which turned out to be a better approach anyway since I can be fully transparent about the methodology.

The main wrangling challenge was reconciling three different time frequencies. I resampled the weekly mortgage data to quarterly averages, forward-filled the annual income into quarterly observations, and merged everything on a shared quarterly index from 1984 to 2024. I then engineered two metrics: a price-to-income ratio and a monthly payment-to-income ratio using the standard amortization formula assuming 20% down on a 30-year fixed loan.

Week 12

Exploratory Analysis & Visualizations

This week I moved from data wrangling into actual exploratory analysis. The goal was to get a feel for the shape and story of the data across all three series before committing to a specific narrative or model. What I found was more striking than I anticipated — affordability hasn't just been trending downward, it has accelerated in ways that stand out even historically.

Key Findings

  • The post-2020 affordability collapse is historically distinct. The early 1980s had worse payment burdens in percentage terms, but those were driven purely by interest rates — prices were low. The 2022–2024 period is the first time in the dataset where both prices and rates were elevated simultaneously, creating a compounding squeeze with no historical parallel to draw on.
  • The 2008 crash gave only temporary relief. The price-to-income ratio fell from roughly 5.7× at the 2006 peak back toward 3.5× by 2012 — a meaningful correction. But the ratio resumed climbing almost immediately, and by 2022 had exceeded the pre-crash peak by a wide margin, making the crash look like a detour rather than a reset.
  • Falling mortgage rates masked the affordability problem for over a decade. From roughly 2010 to 2021, the 30-year rate fell from about 5% to under 3%, keeping monthly payment burdens manageable even as home prices kept climbing. Once rates reversed in 2022, the full scale of the price gap became apparent nearly overnight.
  • Income has grown, but nowhere near fast enough. Real median household income rose from roughly $50,000 to around $80,000 between 1984 and 2024 — a meaningful 60% gain. But median new home prices over the same period rose from about $86,000 to over $400,000, more than 4× the rate of income growth even in nominal terms.
  • The 3× price-to-income benchmark is effectively obsolete. The ratio has not been below 3× since the early 1990s and has not been below 5× since the post-2008 trough. Whether this reflects a structural shift in housing markets, a long-run supply failure, or a bubble is one of the questions I want to explore further.

Limitations

National aggregates only

All three series are U.S.-wide. A family in rural Iowa and one in San Francisco are indistinguishable. My original research questions about city-level variation are still unanswered by this data.

New construction only

MSPUS covers newly built homes, which skew higher-end than the broader resale market. My affordability estimates may be slightly pessimistic relative to what the typical buyer actually encounters.

20% down payment assumed

The payment calculation assumes 20% down — increasingly unrealistic for first-time buyers. Buyers putting down less face higher monthly costs and PMI on top, making the actual burden worse.

Renters and inequality invisible

About 35% of U.S. households rent and are entirely absent from this analysis. The median income figure also hides how affordability may be deteriorating far faster for lower-income households.
